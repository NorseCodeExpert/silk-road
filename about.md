# About This Project 

This digital exhibit explores the Terracotta Army of Qin Shi Huang as more than a funerary monument. Rather than focusing only on its scale or artistic achievement, this project examines how the army reflects the political, military, and administrative systems of the Qin state. These systems—defined by standardization, hierarchy, and coordination—formed the foundation for later developments that made long-distance exchange across Eurasia possible.

Through four selected objects—the standing archer, kneeling archer, chariot with warriors, and armored officer—this project highlights different aspects of Qin military organization. Each figure represents a specific role within a larger system: mobility, precision, command, and authority. Together, they reveal how the Qin empire operated not as a collection of individuals, but as a coordinated structure designed for efficiency and control.

This structure is significant because it helps explain the origins of the Silk Road. The Qin dynasty unified territories, standardized infrastructure, and established centralized governance. These developments created the stability and connectivity later expanded under the Han dynasty, allowing goods, people, and ideas to move across vast distances.

By examining the Terracotta Army in this way, the project argues that large-scale exchange did not emerge suddenly, but was built upon earlier systems of organization and control. The army, buried beneath the earth, serves as a powerful reminder of the structures that made the ancient world increasingly interconnected.
